EDBT/ICDT 2024 Diversity & Inclusion Tutorial

"Interactive tutorial on giving inaccessible, unclear and boring presentations"

by Benny Kimelfeld and Julia Stoyanovich


Abstract: The goal of this tutorial is to raise the awareness of the EDBT/ICDT attendees about the quality of their presentation, focusing on accessibility and diversity of the target audience. The “traditional” qualities that speakers typically aim for involve the effectiveness of the message: the audience should clearly understand the story told, believe it, and care about it (consistently with the ancient rhetorical appeals of logos, ethos, and pathos, respectively). In this tutorial, we will additionally focus on other qualities that apply to a diverse audience, especially at an international scientific conference. For example, we will discuss how making our presentations easier to understand for people with disabilities, such as by diminishing the reliance on high-quality eyesight (use contrasting color and shapes) or hearing (use captions), can help everyone understand the presentation better. We will also discuss that illustrations or jokes one chooses to use in a presentation may come up as offensive to people of different backgrounds. Similarly, some metaphors may overly rely on the cultural background of specific groups (e.g., regional politics, metaphors of local sports, popular television series, and so on), thus will likely be missed by others in the audience, excluding them from the conversation. And we will also discuss that one should be aware of the potential impact of our presentation choices on the endorsement of social wrongs and biases. Finally, we will raise the need to be aware of one’s own accent, which may be difficult for others to grasp, and so it is important to make sure that the slides compensate for this. The tutorial will be interactive, encouraging members of the audience to share tips and experiences, with the group discussion taking up a significant portion of the allotted time.

Benny Kimelfeld is a Professor at the Computer Science Faculty at Technion, Israel, and the head and founder of the Technion Data and Knowledge (TD\&K) Laboratory. After receiving his Ph.D. from The Hebrew University of Jerusalem and before joining Technion, he has been a Research Staff Member at IBM Research Almaden and a Computer Scientist at LogicBlox. Benny's research spans a spectrum of both foundational and systems aspects of data management, such as algorithms for query evaluation, probabilistic and inconsistent databases, and infrastructure for text analytics. His research has been funded by various agencies and programs, including the German-Israeli Project Cooperation (DIP). He served as the Program Committee Chair of ICDT 2018 and he currently serves as an Associate Editor for the Journal of Computer and System Sciences (JCSS) and the Program Committee Chair of PODS 2025.


Julia Stoyanovich is Institute Associate Professor of Computer Science & Engineering, Associate Professor of Data Science, and Director of the Center for Responsible AI (https://r-ai.co/) at New York University. Her goal is to make “responsible AI” synonymous with “AI”. She works towards this goal by engaging in academic research, education and technology policy, and by speaking about the benefits and harms of AI to practitioners and members of the public. Julia's research interests include data management and AI systems, and AI ethics and legal compliance. She has co-authored over 100 academic publications, and has written for the New York Times, the Wall Street Journal and Le Monde. Julia holds a Ph.D. in Computer Science from Columbia University, she is a recipient of the NSF CAREER Award and a Senior Member of the ACM.
