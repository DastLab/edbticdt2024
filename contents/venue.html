Paestum

Located on the coast of the Tyrrhenian Sea in southern Italy, Paestum stands as a testament to ancient Greek civilization in Magna Graecia. Originally founded as Poseidonia around the 6th century BCE by Greek settlers, the city flourished as a thriving center of trade and culture. Its strategic location facilitated connections between the Ionian and Tyrrhenian Seas, contributing to its economic prosperity and political significance.

Throughout its history, Paestum underwent various transformations under different rulers. After being conquered by the Lucanians and later the Romans, it was renamed Paestum. The city's architectural marvels, particularly its three remarkably well-preserved Greek temples dedicated to Poseidon (Neptune), Hera, and Ceres, attest to its ancient grandeur. These temples, constructed in the Doric style, are among the finest examples of ancient Greek architecture in the world. In addition to its religious significance, Paestum was also renowned for its artistic heritage. The archaeological site houses a wealth of treasures, including millennia-old frescoes, ceramics, and artifacts, providing insight into daily life and rituals of the ancient inhabitants. One of the most iconic finds is the Tomb of the Diver, adorned with a captivating funerary fresco depicting a diver plunging into the unknown.

Despite its illustrious past, Paestum faced challenges over the centuries, including outbreaks of malaria and invasions by raiders. The decline of the Roman Empire further contributed to its gradual abandonment. However, the rediscovery of Paestum in the 18th century sparked renewed interest in its rich history and cultural significance.

Today, Paestum is recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site, drawing visitors from around the globe to marvel at its ancient ruins and immerse themselves in its fascinating past. It is part of the city of Capaccio-Paestum in the Province of Salerno, which is is one of the biggest towns in northern Cilento, with a population of 22,802. The city, directly to the south of the archaeological site, is a popular seaside resort with long sandy beaches.
